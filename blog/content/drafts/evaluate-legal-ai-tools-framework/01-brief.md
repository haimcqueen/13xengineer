## Competitor Gaps
Existing content falls into two camps: (1) vendor-produced "frameworks" that are thinly disguised sales pitches (Ironclad's 4 Cs, Harvey's 7 criteria), and (2) generic "best AI tools" listicles that compare surface-level features without a decision-making methodology. Key gaps:

- **No adoption-weighted evaluation**: Nobody weighs adoption rate as a primary evaluation criterion, even though most legal AI tools see under 20% sustained usage. A tool that lawyers refuse to use has zero ROI regardless of its feature set.
- **No workflow integration depth**: Competitor frameworks mention "integration" as a checkbox item but don't explain why inline integration (Word, DMS) matters more than standalone portals for actual lawyer adoption.
- **No pilot design guidance**: Articles say "run a pilot" without specifying what to measure, how long, or what success looks like.
- **Missing change management angle**: Evaluation criteria focus entirely on the technology and ignore organizational readiness, training investment, and partner buy-in.
- **No cost-of-switching analysis**: Existing content treats the evaluation as a one-time purchase decision, ignoring vendor lock-in, data portability, and migration costs.

## Differentiation Statement

### What competitors miss:
Existing evaluation frameworks treat legal AI like enterprise software procurement: compare features, check security boxes, negotiate pricing. They ignore the single biggest predictor of legal AI failure, which is lawyer adoption. A tool can score perfectly on features and security but deliver zero value if 80% of the firm never opens it.

### Our unique angle:
We flip the evaluation hierarchy. Adoption predictors (workflow integration, minimal behavior change, collaborative features) rank above raw capability. The framework is structured around six weighted dimensions with clear scoring guidance, so a managing partner can hand it to a committee and get a defensible recommendation.

### Legora-exclusive insight:
BAHR's 80% active user rate and 30% daily power usage provides a concrete benchmark for what good adoption looks like. Most legal AI vendors cannot cite comparable numbers. This data point grounds the framework in reality rather than theory.
