## Dimension 1: Workflow Integration
**Purpose**: Explain why this is the #1 evaluation criterion, counterintuitive to most buyers who start with features.
**Key points**:
- Lawyers work in Word, Outlook, and document management systems. If the AI requires a separate browser tab, adoption drops fast
- Evaluate: Does the tool have a native Word add-in? Does it connect to your DMS (iManage, SharePoint)? Can lawyers access it without leaving their current application?
- The difference between "has an API" and "works inside Word" is the difference between 20% and 80% adoption
- Example: Legora operates inside Microsoft Word with a native add-in and connects directly to iManage and SharePoint, so lawyers never leave their primary work environment

### What to Score
- Native integrations with Word, Outlook, DMS
- Whether the tool uses firm-specific precedent or generic templates
- Context awareness (does it understand the document being edited?)

## Dimension 2: Adoption & Usability
**Purpose**: Frame adoption as something you evaluate before purchase, not after.
**Key points**:
- Ask vendors for adoption metrics from existing deployments. If they cannot provide them, that is a signal.
- Benchmark: BAHR achieves 80% active user rates with Legora, with 30% using it 10+ times daily. Most legal AI tools see under 20% sustained usage.
- Evaluate the required behavior change: a tool that lives inside existing workflows (Word, DMS) requires less change than a standalone portal
- Run a 30-60 day pilot with 3-5 power users on one specific workflow before committing
- 39% of professionals cite inadequate training as an adoption barrier [VERIFIED] - [Wolters Kluwer](https://www.wolterskluwer.com/en/expert-insights/legal-ai-adoption-time-savings-revenue-growth)

### Pilot Program Design
- Duration: 30-60 days
- Participants: 3-5 power users from one practice area
- Metrics to measure: daily active users, tasks completed, time savings, user satisfaction
- Success criteria: define before starting (e.g., 50%+ daily usage, measurable time savings on target workflow)

## Dimension 3: Accuracy & Reliability
**Purpose**: Explain how to test accuracy beyond the vendor demo.
**Key points**:
- Vendor demos use clean, curated documents. Your firm's documents are messy, multi-jurisdictional, and full of non-standard clauses.
- Test with your own documents during the pilot, not vendor-supplied samples
- AI achieves 90-95% accuracy on standard document elements [VERIFIED] - [Stanford](https://www.allaboutai.com/resources/ai-statistics/ai-in-law/)
- Only 22.1% of legal professionals report high trust in AI outputs [VERIFIED] - [Law.com](https://www.law.com/legaltechnews/2026/03/20/legals-gen-ai-adoption-is-rising-fast-but-trust-and-confidence-lag-far-behind-/)
- Evaluate: Does the tool use your firm's precedent library (like Legora does) or generic training data? Firm-specific intelligence produces more relevant, trustworthy outputs.

### What to Score
- Performance on your actual documents (not demo docs)
- Citation accuracy and source transparency
- Ability to use firm-specific precedent and work product
- Error detection and human review workflow

## Dimension 4: Security & Compliance
**Purpose**: Cover the table-stakes requirements. Not a differentiator, but a disqualifier.
**Key points**:
- 39% of firms cite data privacy as a top concern [VERIFIED] - [Wolters Kluwer](https://www.wolterskluwer.com/en/expert-insights/legal-ai-adoption-time-savings-revenue-growth)
- Minimum requirements: SOC 2 Type II, data isolation (your data is not used to train the model), encryption at rest and in transit, SSO, audit trails
- Ask explicitly: "Is our client data used for model training?" The answer must be no.
- Contractual data processing agreements specifying data access, retention, and deletion policies
- On-premise vs. cloud deployment options for highly sensitive work

### Security Evaluation Checklist
- SOC 2 Type II certification
- Data isolation guarantees (contractual)
- Zero data retention for model training
- SSO and role-based access control
- Audit trails and activity logging
- Encryption (at rest and in transit)
- GDPR/CCPA compliance
- Incident response plan

## Dimension 5: Total Cost of Ownership
**Purpose**: Expand the cost conversation beyond the subscription price.
**Key points**:
- Sticker price is misleading. Include: licensing, implementation, training, integration, ongoing support, and switching costs
- Harvey: $100-$1,200/user/month with 25-50 seat minimums ($30K-$300K+/year). CoCounsel: $110-$400/user/month. Luminance: custom enterprise pricing.
- Calculate cost per adopted user, not cost per licensed seat. A $200/user tool with 80% adoption costs less per productive user than a $100/user tool with 15% adoption.
- Factor in switching costs and vendor lock-in: how portable is your data if you change tools?

**Include**:

| Cost Category | What to Ask | Hidden Traps |
|---------------|-------------|--------------|
| Licensing | Per-user or per-seat? Seat minimums? | Volume commitments that lock you in |
| Implementation | Who does the setup? How long? | Underestimated integration timelines |
| Training | Included or extra? Ongoing or one-time? | 54% of firms provide no AI training |
| Integration | DMS, SSO, custom workflows? | API-only "integration" vs. native |
| Switching | Data export? Contract lock-in period? | Proprietary formats, long contracts |

## Dimension 6: Vendor Partnership
**Purpose**: Evaluate the vendor as a long-term partner, not just a product.
**Key points**:
- Legal AI is evolving fast. The vendor you choose today needs to keep pace for 3-5 years.
- Evaluate: responsiveness to feature requests, transparency about limitations, willingness to customize for your firm's needs
- Ask for references from firms of similar size and practice area
- Red flags: no adoption metrics available, refusal to run a pilot, aggressive seat-minimum requirements

### What to Score
- Customer references (ask for firms like yours)
- Roadmap transparency
- Support responsiveness and quality
- Customization willingness
- Contract flexibility (pilot periods, exit clauses)

## FAQ Section

**What criteria should law firms use to evaluate AI tools?**
Answer: Focus on six dimensions: workflow integration depth, adoption potential (ask for metrics from existing clients), accuracy on your firm's real documents, security architecture (SOC 2, data isolation), total cost of ownership (not just licensing), and vendor partnership quality. Weight integration and adoption higher than feature lists, because a tool that lawyers refuse to use has zero ROI.

**How do you measure ROI of legal AI software?**
Answer: Calculate cost per adopted user, not cost per licensed seat. Track time savings on specific workflows (document review, research, drafting), measure adoption rate after 90 days, and compare against the cost of the traditional process. The Wolters Kluwer 2026 survey found that 62% of AI-using professionals save 6-20% of their weekly time, and 52% reported proportional revenue increases.

**How long does a legal AI pilot program take?**
Answer: Plan for 30-60 days with 3-5 power users focused on one workflow (e.g., contract review or legal research). Define success metrics before starting: daily active users, time saved per task, user satisfaction scores. Firm-wide rollout typically takes 90-120 days after a successful pilot.

**What security requirements should legal AI tools meet?**
Answer: At minimum: SOC 2 Type II certification, contractual guarantees that client data is not used for model training, encryption at rest and in transit, SSO integration, role-based access control, and audit trails. Ask for the SOC 2 report directly; do not accept verbal assurances.

**What is the difference between general AI and legal-specific AI?**
Answer: General AI tools (like ChatGPT) work from broad internet training data and cannot access legal databases, firm precedent, or document management systems. Legal-specific platforms like Legora, Harvey, and CoCounsel are trained on legal-grade datasets, integrate with legal infrastructure (Westlaw, iManage, SharePoint), and include safeguards against hallucinated citations. The accuracy difference matters: AI trained on legal data achieves 90-95% accuracy on standard document elements vs. significantly lower rates from general tools.

**Why do most legal AI implementations fail?**
Answer: The primary cause is the adoption gap. Firms buy tools based on feature demos without evaluating whether lawyers will change their behavior to use them. When the AI lives in a separate application, usage drops off within weeks. Tools that integrate into Word and existing document management systems require less behavior change and sustain higher adoption rates.
