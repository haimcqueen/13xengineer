## FAQ Section

**What should be included in outside counsel guidelines?**
Answer: Modern OCGs cover five core areas: resourcing and staffing requirements, permissible activities and billing codes, commercial terms and rate caps, billing format and submission rules, and AI governance provisions (disclosure, data security, fee adjustments). The ACC publishes sample guidelines that serve as a starting template.

**How do you enforce outside counsel billing guidelines?**
Answer: Most legal departments now use e-billing software that automatically validates invoices against OCG rules, checking rate cards, task codes, staffing limits, and submission deadlines. The 2025 Legal Bill Review survey found that 87% of in-house teams spend four hours or less per month on manual bill review, making automation essential.

**How are outside counsel guidelines changing with AI?**
Answer: Corporate clients are adding AI-specific provisions covering four areas: disclosure requirements (firms must inform clients when AI is used), data security rules (prohibiting confidential data on public AI platforms), quality assurance standards (human review of AI outputs), and billing adjustments (AI efficiencies should lower costs, not be billed as separate line items).

**What is the difference between outside counsel guidelines and billing guidelines?**
Answer: Billing guidelines are a subset of outside counsel guidelines. OCGs cover the full relationship: staffing, communication, matter management, AI use, diversity requirements, and conflicts. Billing guidelines specifically address invoicing rules, rate caps, expense policies, and fee arrangements.

**How do law firms manage outside counsel guideline compliance?**
Answer: Leading firms assign a dedicated legal operations or compliance team to review incoming OCGs, map requirements to internal processes, and configure their billing and practice management systems accordingly. AI platforms like Legora help by embedding compliant workflows (precedent-based drafting, efficient document review) directly into the tools lawyers already use.

**Can law firms charge clients for AI tool usage?**
Answer: The ACC's sample guidelines state that if an AI tool "supports a standard part of the lawyer's legal practice, it should not be expensed to a client." Standalone AI costs may qualify as reimbursable expenses, but the trend is toward AI being treated as overhead that reduces overall fees rather than a billable add-on.

**How does AI help law firms comply with stricter outside counsel guidelines?**
Answer: AI reduces document review time from weeks to hours and research time from hours to minutes, helping firms stay within client budgets. Enterprise AI platforms also provide the governance infrastructure (audit trails, data isolation, usage policies) that clients increasingly require in their OCGs.
