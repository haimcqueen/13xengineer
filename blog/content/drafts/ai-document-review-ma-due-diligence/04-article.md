## A $1.5 Million Lesson in AI Due Diligence

During the acquisition of a manufacturing supplier, an AI tool analyzing financial statements confidently reported that a 2022 real estate sale was tax-compliant. It cited a specific tax declaration document. The document didn't exist. Post-close, the buyer discovered a [$1.5 million tax liability](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/legal-exchange-insights-and-commentary/ais-due-diligence-applications-need-rigorous-human-oversight) that the AI had effectively hidden behind a fabricated reference.

This is the state of AI document review for M&A due diligence in 2026. Sullivan & Cromwell's [January memo](https://www.sullcrom.com/insights/memo/2026/January/Use-AI-Tools-Mergers-Acquisitions-Transactions) on AI in M&A transactions puts it plainly: inaccurate, incomplete, or biased GenAI output is "routinely observed." Three months later, [S&C itself apologized](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/top-law-firm-apologizes-to-bankruptcy-judge-for-ai-hallucination) to a federal bankruptcy judge for AI hallucinations in a court filing.

And yet: [McKinsey's 2025 survey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/m-and-a/our-insights/gen-ai-in-m-and-a-from-theory-to-practice-to-high-performance) found that firms using AI in M&A see 20% cost reductions and 30-50% faster deal cycles. A mid-market acquisition that used to require six to eight weeks of document review can now close in ten to fourteen days. The tools work. They just don't work the way most people assume they do, and the gap between demo-day performance and live-deal reality is where deals get expensive.

This is the guide to what actually delivers results, what breaks under pressure, and how to implement AI document review without becoming the next cautionary tale.

## What AI Document Review Does in a Live M&A Deal

Forget the marketing slides. Here's what AI tools actually handle in a data room with thousands of contracts.

**Bulk clause extraction.** The AI pulls change-of-control provisions, assignment restrictions, termination rights, and indemnification caps across the entire document set. A deal with 3,000 contracts that would take a team of associates weeks gets processed in hours. Before AI, lawyers reviewed [50 to 100 documents per hour](https://www.spellbook.legal/learn/ai-due-diligence). With the right tools, that number reaches several thousand.

**Cross-document pattern detection.** This is where AI earns its keep. Individual contract review misses patterns that only surface when you look across the full data room. Inconsistent termination rights across vendor agreements. Change-of-control triggers scattered across employment contracts, facility leases, and IP licenses. An AI tool running tabular analysis across the entire set catches these in a single pass.

**Risk flagging.** The AI identifies non-standard language, missing clauses, and provisions that deviate from market terms. Instead of reading every paragraph of every contract, attorneys get a prioritized list of issues to investigate.

**Structured output.** The result is a queryable tabular view of the entire document set, not a 200-page memo that nobody reads. Partners can filter by issue type, sort by risk level, and drill into specific documents.

### The Accuracy Spectrum

Here's the part most vendor content skips. AI document review accuracy varies depending on what it's looking at.

Standard clauses get 90-95% accuracy. Change of control, assignment, termination, non-compete, confidentiality. The AI has seen millions of these and recognizes them reliably. [Kira Systems](https://www.litera.com/products/kira) reports 90%+ accuracy across 1,400 pre-trained clause models, backed by 45,000 hours of lawyer training data.

Non-standard or bespoke provisions drop to 75-85%. Jurisdiction-specific regulatory clauses. Unusual earn-out structures. Custom indemnification baskets. The AI still finds these, but it mischaracterizes them more often.

The problem is that most tools don't flag when they're operating in the lower-accuracy zone. An associate reviewing flagged items doesn't know which extractions are high-confidence and which are best guesses. This is where the $1.5 million mistakes happen.

## Where AI Document Review Breaks

Being honest about the failure modes matters more than listing features. These are the three ways AI document review goes wrong in M&A.

### Hallucinations in High-Stakes Contexts

GenAI tools fabricate. They cite non-existent documents, mischaracterize provisions, and generate analysis that reads convincingly but is factually wrong. The $1.5 million tax liability case is one example. Sullivan & Cromwell's own court filing debacle in April 2026 is another.

The fix is not to avoid AI. The fix is to never treat AI output as verified work product. AI suggests and flags. Attorneys validate and approve. This is what [ABA Formal Opinion 512](https://www.americanbar.org/news/abanews/aba-news-archives/2024/07/aba-issues-first-ethics-guidance-ai-tools/) requires: lawyers must understand the tool's capabilities and limitations before using it for client work.

### The Adoption Problem Nobody Talks About

Here's a stat that should concern anyone evaluating legal AI: firm-wide adoption of generative AI tools [dropped from 24% to 21%](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/law_practice/resources/tech-report/2024/2024-artificial-intelligence-techreport/) between 2023 and 2024, according to the ABA TechReport. Individual lawyers are experimenting, but institutional adoption is stalling.

Meanwhile, on the client side, [86% of corporate and PE leaders](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/what-we-do/capabilities/mergers-acquisitions-restructuring/articles/m-and-a-generative-ai-study.html) have integrated GenAI into M&A workflows, per Deloitte. Client-side AI adoption is outpacing law firm adoption by roughly 4:1. Your clients are already using these tools. They're starting to expect you to as well.

Why do most legal AI tools fail on adoption? They require lawyers to leave Word, leave their DMS, learn a new interface, and change how they work. Lawyers are busy. They won't do that for marginal efficiency gains. The tools that achieve real adoption are the ones that embed into the existing workflow.

At BAHR, one of the leading Nordic law firms, [Legora](https://legora.ai) achieved 80% active user adoption, with 30% of lawyers using it 10+ times per day. That's 4x the industry average. The difference is that Legora lives inside Word, iManage, and SharePoint. No new interface. No context-switching. Attorneys use AI capabilities without changing how they work.

### The Ethics Tension

ABA Formal Opinion 512 cuts both ways. Lawyers must verify AI output before treating it as work product. But courts are also starting to ask whether manual-only review pipelines meet the competence standard when better tools exist. The ethical floor is rising: you can't blindly trust AI, and you increasingly can't ignore it either.

## Implementing AI Document Review Without Getting Burned

Four principles that separate successful implementations from expensive failures.

**Pick a tool that lives in your existing stack.** If it requires a new login, a new tab, and training sessions, expect low adoption. The firms that get value from AI document review are the ones where lawyers encounter the AI inside Word, their DMS, or their existing review interface. Legora's approach of embedding directly into Word, iManage, and SharePoint is why BAHR hit 80% adoption while most firms struggle past 20%.

**Human-in-the-loop is non-negotiable.** AI extracts, suggests, and flags. Attorneys validate and approve. Every time. The Sullivan & Cromwell examples (both the memo warning about it and the April 2026 filing that proved it) make this crystal clear. Treat AI output as a first draft, not a finished product.

**Train on your firm's precedent.** Generic clause models miss firm-specific standards. A platform like Legora that uses the firm's own work product as training data catches provisions that a generic model would mischaracterize, because it knows how your firm structures indemnification baskets, how your partners phrase termination triggers, and what your clients consider market.

**Measure adoption, not just capability.** A tool with 95% accuracy and 15% adoption delivers less value than a tool with 90% accuracy and 80% adoption. Track active user rates and engagement frequency alongside traditional metrics like time-to-completion and issues caught. If your adoption rate is below 30%, the tool is failing regardless of its specifications.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can AI replace lawyers in M&A due diligence?**
No. AI handles bulk extraction and pattern detection that would take a team of associates weeks. Attorneys make the judgment calls: which flagged issues are material, how risks interact across the deal, and what the client needs to know. ABA Formal Opinion 512 requires human oversight of all AI-generated work product.

**How accurate is AI document review for M&A?**
Current tools achieve 90-95% accuracy on standard clause types (change of control, assignment, termination). Accuracy drops to 75-85% on unusual or bespoke provisions. The best approach is to use AI for first-pass extraction and flagging, then have attorneys verify the results, particularly for non-standard clauses.

**How much time does AI save in due diligence?**
McKinsey's 2025 survey found that AI-assisted due diligence cuts deal timelines by 30-50%. A mid-market acquisition requiring 6-8 weeks of manual document review can be completed in 10-14 days with AI tools. Firms also report average cost reductions of 20%.

**What are the risks of using AI in M&A due diligence?**
The primary risks are hallucinations (AI fabricating document references or mischaracterizing provisions), accuracy drops on non-standard clauses, and over-reliance without attorney verification. Sullivan & Cromwell's January 2026 memo warns that inaccurate GenAI output is "routinely observed." Mitigation requires human-in-the-loop workflows and tools trained on verified legal data.

**What are the best AI tools for legal due diligence?**
Leading platforms include Legora (workflow integration and bulk document analysis), Harvey (general legal AI with custom agents), Kira by Litera (mature clause extraction with 90%+ accuracy), and Luminance (pattern detection and anomaly flagging). The best choice depends on your firm's existing tech stack, deal volume, and whether you need specialized due diligence capabilities or a general-purpose legal AI.

**Is AI document review ethical for M&A work?**
Yes, with proper safeguards. ABA Formal Opinion 512 permits AI use but requires lawyers to understand the tool's capabilities and limitations before deploying it on client work. The ethical obligation runs in both directions: lawyers must verify AI output, and courts are beginning to question whether manual-only review meets competence standards when proven AI tools exist.
