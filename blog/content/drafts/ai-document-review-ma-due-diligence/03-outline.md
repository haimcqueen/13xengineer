## FAQ Section

**Can AI replace lawyers in M&A due diligence?**
No. AI handles the bulk extraction and pattern detection that would take a team of associates weeks. Attorneys still make the judgment calls: which flagged issues are material, how risks interact, and what the client needs to know. ABA Formal Opinion 512 requires human oversight of all AI-generated work product.

**How accurate is AI document review for M&A?**
Current tools achieve 90-95% accuracy on standard clause types (change of control, assignment, termination). Accuracy drops to 75-85% on unusual or bespoke provisions. The best approach: use AI for first-pass extraction and flagging, then have attorneys verify the results, especially for non-standard clauses.

**How much time does AI save in due diligence?**
McKinsey's 2025 survey found that AI-assisted due diligence cuts deal timelines by 30-50%. A mid-market acquisition that previously required 6-8 weeks of document review can be completed in 10-14 days. Firms also report average cost reductions of 20%.

**What are the risks of using AI in M&A due diligence?**
The primary risks are hallucinations (AI fabricating document references or mischaracterizing provisions), accuracy drops on non-standard clauses, and over-reliance without attorney verification. Sullivan & Cromwell's January 2026 memo warns that inaccurate GenAI output is "routinely observed." Mitigation requires human-in-the-loop workflows and tools trained on verified legal data.

**What are the best AI tools for legal due diligence?**
Leading platforms include Legora (strong on workflow integration and bulk document analysis), Harvey (general legal AI with custom agents), Kira by Litera (mature clause extraction with 90%+ accuracy), and Luminance (pattern detection and anomaly flagging). The best choice depends on your firm's existing tools, deal volume, and whether you need a specialized due diligence platform or a general-purpose legal AI.

**Is AI document review ethical for M&A work?**
Yes, when used with proper safeguards. ABA Formal Opinion 512 permits AI use but requires lawyers to understand the tool's capabilities and limitations. The ethical obligation cuts both ways: while lawyers must verify AI output, courts are also beginning to ask whether manual-only review meets competence standards when proven AI tools exist.
